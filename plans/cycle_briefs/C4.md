# Cycle 4 brief — Writer, WASM, release engineering; R2 ships; **the Q3 checkpoint**

**Layers:** L5–L6 (partial) · **Envelope:** **R2 — Interoperable Writer Core ships** · **Kernel-touch expectation:** `none`

This is the cycle where the build pauses and the evidence assembles. It has two halves, and the second half is not engineering.

---

## 1. Scope boundary — build half

**In.** Draft roots and the builder API, with authoring roots kept type-distinct from parsed-document roots (§22.0). Canonical and preservation serializers (§22.4). Incremental updates and source-preserving append (§23.3). Object allocation, trailer identity, cross-reference strategy, object streams, and stream encoding on the writer side (§22.3, §22.6–22.7). Font subsetting and embedding, with `ToUnicode` generation (§20.17, §22.10). **Structural signature discovery and impact classification** — `ByteRange`, DocMDP/FieldMDP/lock classification (§16.5). The external-reader matrix. The round-trip metamorphic gauntlet (§27/G4). The **WASM surface** (§25.5), which unblocks the Observatory playground C3 seeded. **Release engineering begins** — reproducible builds, packaging, documentation, and the G6.1 supply-chain lanes — so the wedge is installable by a customer rather than buildable by its authors.

**Out.** Cryptographic and trust validation of signatures — CMS verification, DSS/VRI, timestamps, revocation — is C5. C4 discovers and classifies signature *structure* and the *impact* an edit would have on it; it renders no trust verdict. Encryption *output* (the secure-writer profile, D1R re-encryption) is C5. Canonical rewrite with carry/drop maps, sanitization, and redaction are C5. Linearized output and structured authoring are later.

**Why the signature split, precisely.** This is the R2-N1 envelope fix, and it is worth stating in full because it is a live instance of the bug class §34.4 forbids. Workflow F and §23.3 require structural signature analysis for R2's incremental writes — you cannot honestly append to a signed document without knowing what the signature covers and what your append breaks. Workflow B promises signature *structure* at R0. But the audit found the capability sitting only in R3. So: structural discovery and impact classification land here, at R2, where the writer needs them; cryptographic trust validation stays at C5, where it belongs. The two are different claims with different falsifiers, and merging them is exactly how a writer silently invalidates a signature and calls it success.

## 3. Scope — evidence half (the Q3 checkpoint)

The build pauses. Charter §5/C4 assembles: equal-resource **Baselines A–D** (plus E, expert comparison, where feasible); mechanism **ablations**; **red-team results** from the window that has been running since day 0; the **Evolution Trial (Flagship F)** run formally against the R2 artifact; and the **ledger to date**. The package goes to independent review on ~day 9. The verdict pends on the reviewers' calendar and the campaign does not wait for it (Charter §2/G2, §8 risk 6).

**Four laws the checkpoint must not violate.**

- **§33.5.1 fairness.** Every comparison freezes the same task statement, source pack, repository exposure, model and tool access, compute, elapsed feedback, human attention, attempt budget, stopping rule, and held-out boundary — everything except the mechanism under study. The control receives the *strongest practical ordinary alternative*, not a strawman. Nominal and consumed resources are both reported. No condition author is sole judge.
- **§33.6 ablations are interventions, not demolition.** Remove one mechanism; supply the strongest ordinary replacement; report null and negative results.
- **R2-N5.** Baselines C and D (repository-level) must be disambiguated from the §33.6 ablations (mechanism-level) in one explicit sentence. One experiment may not be counted as two evidence classes.
- **§33.8 distribution.** Attempts, success rate, median and percentiles, best-of-N, retries, interventions, cost, defect distribution, representative failures, discarded branches. One spectacular branch proves reachability; reliability requires the distribution.

**The budget is the risk.** Charter §8 risk 1 names baseline cost concentration the most lethal risk in the campaign: multiple full vanilla-agent rebuild attempts at equal budget, the most expensive and least enjoyable item on the list, and the one whose quiet omission silently selects FrankenSim's outcome — because without baselines there is *zero* disproportion evidence. The budget is reserved at day 0. A cut is a formal downgrade of goal G2, recorded as such, never a quiet omission.

## 7. Known risks

1. **Baseline budget concentration** (Charter §8 risk 1) — see §3. The only mitigation is that the money was committed on day 0.
2. **The checkpoint competes with the wedge.** Charter §2's degradation law is explicit: evidence apparatus may consume cycle capacity, but it may never sit on the wedge's critical path. If C4 slips, R2 ships and the checkpoint moves — not the reverse.
3. **Signature-impact classification is where a writer silently destroys evidence.** An incremental write that breaks a signature and reports success is a Grade-A correctness failure with legal consequences for a user.
4. **WASM's capability manifest invites a hidden fallback.** §35 forbids one. A codec silently absent in the WASM build, with the Rust build's claim matrix reused, is a truth-drift defect of exactly the R1-1 class.
5. **Kernel-touch pressure peaks here.** The writer introduces draft roots and transform roots into the identity graph. If the kernel was extracted wrong at C0, this is where it shows — and the migration cost is paid at C4 rather than absorbed.
